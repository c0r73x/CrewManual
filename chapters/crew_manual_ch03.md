# 3. CREW & COMMS

The crew is not at the character's side. They are voices in the radio. They are stationed elsewhere — on the bridge, in engineering, at the airlock, in sickbay — and the character reaches them only through comms. They can help, but only at a distance, and only when they answer.

This chapter covers how to build the crew, how to talk to them, and what happens when they go silent.

## 3.1 Building The Crew

A standard crew is three to five members. Fewer than three feels thin. More than five becomes administration.

Each crew member needs:

- **Name.** A working surname is enough. Crews use them.
- **Role.** What they do aboard. Engineer, Medic, Marine, Scientist, Comms Officer, Captain.
- **Station.** Where they normally are. Section name and a specific spot.
- **Trait.** One sentence on how they speak and behave.
- **Key Skills.** One or two Mothership skills they are good at. These are what the character borrows when calling them.

That is enough. Crew members are not full Mothership characters. They have no inventory, no Stress, no health to track in the standard way. They are *assets with voices*.

> *EXAMPLE.* **Voss, Engineer.** Stationed in main engineering, lower decks. Terse, hates corporate, swears in Russian. Skills: Engineering 50, Mechanical Repair 40.

## 3.3 What Crew Can Do

Crew members help in four ways. The character must invoke them through comms (3.4).

**Skill Loans.** When the character needs a skill they do not have, the crew member at the relevant station rolls instead. The character describes what they need; the crew rolls their skill at their stat.

> *EXAMPLE.* The character is at a sealed terminal but is not a Computers specialist. They call Voss. Voss talks them through a manual interface bypass. Voss rolls Engineering. Success — the terminal lights up.

**Information.** The crew has access to records, sensors, logs, and protocols the character does not. Asking them for information costs Battery for the call but no skill check, unless the information requires interpretation.

**Off-Screen Action.** The crew can act at their station while the character is elsewhere. "Voss, reroute power to Section 3" happens *while* the character moves. The action and the player's scene resolve in parallel. The crew reports the result over comms when complete.

**Moral Support.** Hearing a familiar voice steadies the nerves. Once per scene, if a crew member responds to a comms attempt before the character's first Panic Check, the character may reduce Stress by 1d4. This represents the genuine relief of contact, not encouragement.

## 3.4 The Comms Procedure

To contact crew:

1. **Spend 1 Battery charge.** No roll yet — just spent. Then roll Battery die per Chapter 2.
2. **Oracle check: does the crew respond?** Mythic Likelihood: *Likely* by default. Modify by signal conditions:
   - Adjacent section with no compromise: Near Sure Thing
   - Multiple sections away or partial signal degradation: 50/50
   - Damaged comms infrastructure or distant station: Unlikely
   - Active interference, AI compromise, or hostile environment: Very Unlikely
3. **Resolve the response.**
   - **Yes:** The crew member is reachable. Narrate the exchange. Resolve any skill rolls or information requests.
   - **No:** Static, no response, or partial transmission. The character does not know why.
   - **Exceptional Yes / No:** Treat as significant per Mythic.
4. **Random Event check.** If the d10 result on the oracle was a 1, roll a Random Event from the table in *Random Events*.

A single comms exchange is one scene element — not a whole scene. The character can call, get a response, and act on it within a single scene. But each call costs a Charge.

> *EXAMPLE.* The character is in Section 4, dealing with a leak. They call Voss in Engineering (one section over). Spend 1 Charge. Battery die rolls a 4 — no step-down. Oracle: Likely → Yes. Voss responds: "I see the pressure drop on my panel. I can divert flow if you can close manual valve 4-B." The character moves to the valve. Total cost: 1 Charge. Total dice rolled: Battery, oracle, optional Engineering check on Voss's roll.

## 3.5 Crew Position And Movement

Crew members are not always at their default stations. They move. Where they are at any given moment determines what they can do.

Track each crew member's current location in pencil on the worksheet. Update when they move.

Crew movement is driven by:

- **Player request.** The character asks them to go somewhere, do something elsewhere.
- **Crew initiative.** A crew member acts on their own — investigates an alarm, responds to an emergency, follows orders from another crew member.
- **Random event.** Some Random Events specify crew movement.
- **Lockdown.** A station lockdown may force crew into specific sections (see *Station Protocols*).

When the character calls a crew member, the oracle determines whether they are at their expected location. If unsure, ask: *Is [name] still at [station]?* Mythic Likelihood depends on time elapsed and recent events.

A crew member out of position cannot perform their station's functions. A Captain off the bridge cannot issue command overrides. An Engineer away from main engineering cannot reroute power directly.

## 3.6 Crew Stress

Crew members are also subject to fear. They have a simplified Stress track: **Steady → Strained → Breaking → Lost.**

- **Steady:** Default. Crew responds to requests normally.
- **Strained:** Crew responds, but with hesitation, complaint, or reduced reliability. Skill rolls take a -10 penalty.
- **Breaking:** Crew may refuse requests, give bad information, or make unilateral decisions (oracle determines). Skill rolls at -20.
- **Lost:** Crew is no longer reliably an ally. They may abandon their post, lock themselves in, attack the character, or simply stop responding.

Crew Stress steps up when:

- A fellow crew member dies or goes silent (+1 step).
- The crew member's section is directly threatened (+1 step).
- The character makes an unreasonable or impossible request (+1 step).
- A Random Event specifies crew stress.

Crew Stress steps down through:

- Successful resolution of a major threat (-1 step).
- Reuniting with the character or another crew member.
- Time in a safe environment.

The character does not know each crew member's exact Stress state. They infer from voice, hesitation, and behavior. The oracle decides what the crew member sounds like.

> *Crew Stress is a separate, simplified track. It does not use the standard Mothership Panic Check mechanic — only the Steady / Strained / Breaking / Lost states above. Reserve PSG → Stress & Panic for the player character's own Stress.*

## 3.7 Crew Resources

The crew has its own resource state, tracked abstractly. The character does not see crew resource dice. Instead, when the character contacts crew, the crew member may report shortages or constraints.

This is information delivered through dialogue. *"We're running low on power up here."* *"I've used my last sealing patch."* *"The bridge is on emergency rations."*

The Warden — or in solo play, the oracle — decides when and how to reveal crew shortages. Use them to:

- Make crew assistance feel costly (the crew has to give something up to help).
- Add pressure that the character cannot directly address.
- Foreshadow a coming failure.

The character cannot verify what the crew tells them. A crew member at Strained Stress may report shortages that aren't real, or hide ones that are.

## 3.8 When Crew Goes Silent

A crew member who does not respond to comms is not necessarily dead. They might be:

- Out of comms range
- In a section with damaged comms infrastructure
- Behind a closed channel due to lockdown
- Busy with an emergency
- Asleep on shift change
- Compromised — taken, possessed, infected, turned
- Dead

The character does not know which. The first reaction to silence is rarely the correct one.

When a crew member fails to respond, mark them **Silent** on the worksheet. They remain Silent until:

- They respond to a future contact attempt.
- Another crew member confirms their status.
- The character physically reaches their location.

A Silent crew member should not be assumed dead until verified. Reaching them may be a major arc of the session.

> *EXAMPLE.* The character calls the bridge for the third time. No response. The bridge has been Silent for two scenes. The character must decide: keep moving toward their objective, or detour to the bridge to find out what happened. The detour costs time and resources. The objective may not wait.

## 3.9 The Comms Log

Maintain a written log of comms exchanges as the session progresses. Two columns:

- **In:** What crew members say to the character.
- **Out:** What the character says to crew.

The log is not for the character. It is for the player. It preserves voice, builds NPC personality across sessions, and produces a session artifact that captures the texture of remote contact better than session notes alone.

Keep entries short — comms is not novel-writing. Single lines. Fragments. The texture of radio chatter.

> *EXAMPLE.*
> 
> | In                                              | Out                          |
> | ----------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------- |
> | Voss: "What."                                   | "Need power to deck four."   |
> | Voss: "Cost you. Bridge already pulling."       | "Do it anyway."              |
> | Voss: "On your head, then."                     | —                            |

This is the diegetic record of the session. Some players type it. Others handwrite. Either works. The point is the discipline.
