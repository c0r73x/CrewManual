# 14. RESOURCE RECOVERY

Chapter 2 introduced Resource Dice and noted that recovery is never free. This chapter details how each resource is recovered. Every resource has multiple recovery methods, each with its own access requirements, time cost, and consequences. The character cannot expect any single method to always be available. Recovery — like everything else in this manual — is a series of choices.

## 14.1 Overview

A recovery method has four properties:

- **Access:** what the character must have or reach to use it
- **Restoration:** how much resource is recovered (full default die, or one step up)
- **Time:** how long the character is committed to the recovery
- **Consequence:** what costs or risks the recovery imposes

The character's worksheet should track which recovery options are available where on the station. A captain's quarters with a recharge station is a known anchor; a discovered ammo cache is a marked location; a med bay with full kit is a destination. The map of *where the character can survive* is built from these.

A recovered resource resets to its target die size. The character cannot stockpile beyond the default. They carry what they carry.

## 14.2 Battery Recovery

The character's Battery die is the most frequently consumed resource. Four recovery methods.

**Recharge Station.** Fixed installations in specific sections — engineering bays, captain's quarters, life pods, secured operations centers. Restores Battery to default (d8). Time: 10 to 15 minutes. The character is stationary during the charge; tools and weapons are connected to the station. Hearing is reduced by the station's hum.

Requires section to have at least Brownout power. A Dead section's recharge station does not function. Higher-clearance areas may have higher-priority charging that is faster (5 minutes); these are typically Officer or Executive only.

*Consequence:* extended exposure in one location. Random Event triggers are appropriate during recharge — something approaches, an alarm sounds, a crew member arrives unexpectedly.

**Emergency Pack.** Disposable battery packs carried in field equipment, found in caches, taken from fallen marines or technicians. Single use. Restores Battery one step up (d4 → d6, d6 → d8, etc.). Time: under one minute — connect, transfer, discard. The pack is consumed.

Emergency packs are loot. The character finds them in scenes, prioritizes them when carrying capacity is limited, hoards them when situations get bad. A character with three emergency packs in their kit has options that one without packs does not.

*Consequence:* nominal — pack is used, no further effect. Useful in pressure situations where time is the constraint.

**Salvage.** Cannibalize power from another system or device. Common targets: an offline tool, a damaged suit, a crew member's kit (with permission or otherwise), a hardpoint installation no longer in use. Restores Battery one step up. Time: 1 to 3 minutes. Engineering or Computers skill check; failure consumes the time but not the source — the character may try again or abandon.

A successful salvage damages or fully drains the source system. A salvaged tool is no longer functional. A salvaged suit cannot be used. A salvaged installation may have downstream effects on the station's operations.

*Consequence:* the source is gone. The character may be cannibalizing something they will need later.

**Crew Transfer.** A crew member sends power from their station to a designated panel, where the character connects directly. Requires the character to reach a transfer panel — usually one or two per major section, marked on schematics if available. The crew member's section drops one Power tier for the duration of the transfer. Restores Battery 1 to 2 steps depending on transfer length and crew sacrifice.

Comms required to coordinate. The crew member must have access to engineering or reactor controls, or a transfer panel themselves. A captain on the bridge cannot transfer power directly; an engineer in the reactor room can.

*Consequence:* crew section drops a tier. May trigger cascade if section was already degraded. The crew member may take Stress from the sacrifice, especially if their section becomes uncomfortable or dangerous as a result.

## 14.3 Oxygen Recovery

O2 recovery applies primarily during EVA operations. Inside a sealed station section with breathable atmosphere, the O2 die is not actively tracked.

**Tank Swap at Airlock.** The most common method. Spent tanks are exchanged for full ones at airlock-adjacent storage. Restores O2 to default (d10). Time: 1 to 2 minutes — disconnect, swap, connect, pressure test.

Requires the airlock and adjacent storage to be functional. Some airlocks have only the tanks the previous user left; the character may find a station's airlock with no spare tanks at all.

*Consequence:* normally none. In compromised situations, tank swap may be interrupted by lockdown engaging, atmospheric loss in the airlock, or the tank itself being defective.

**Auxiliary Tank From Emergency Pack.** A small backup tank carried in the suit or as separate equipment. Single use. Restores O2 one step up. Time: under 30 seconds.

These are short-term — a typical auxiliary tank holds 15 to 30 minutes of breathing supply. They buy a scene or two of additional time, not a full operation.

*Consequence:* none beyond consumption.

**Suit Transfer.** Take O2 from another suit. The other suit must be intact and contain O2. Common sources: a fallen crew member's suit, a stored backup suit, a discarded suit at an airlock. Restores O2 one step up. Time: 2 to 5 minutes — careful disconnection, transfer line setup, pressurization.

Requires Engineering or Computers skill check at moderate difficulty. Failure may damage the source suit, the character's own suit, or both.

*Consequence:* the source suit becomes unusable for future operations. If the source was a fallen crew member, this is a final action — they cannot be recovered for resuscitation after this.

**Crew Transfer (Lifeline).** In multi-structure stations or stations with lifeline cables, crew at a connected position can route O2 through the lifeline. Requires hardline connection (not radio). Restores O2 1 to 2 steps depending on duration. Time: 3 to 10 minutes during which the character is stationary and connected.

Comms required throughout. The crew member must have access to oxygen reserves and a lifeline connection point.

*Consequence:* drains crew O2 reserves. May trigger crew Stress increase. The lifeline itself becomes a vulnerability — if it fouls or is severed, both ends face consequences.

## 14.4 Ammunition Recovery

Ammo recovery is per weapon — a pistol's magazines and a shotgun's shells are tracked on separate dice. Recovery options vary by weapon type.

**Ammo Cache.** Pre-stocked supplies in armory, security stations, marine quarters, or specific tactical positions. Restores Ammo for one weapon to default. Time: 2 to 5 minutes — locate the right caliber, load magazines, secure.

Often clearance-locked. Marine or Officer clearance for primary armories; Crew clearance may suffice for emergency caches near airlocks.

*Consequence:* caches are limited. Repeated use depletes them. A station with a recently raided cache may have nothing the next time the character returns.

**Looted Ammo.** Take ammunition from another character, crew member, hostile force, or stored body. Restoration depends on what was being carried — typically one to two steps up. Time: 1 to 3 minutes of search.

Requires compatible caliber. A character carrying a pistol cannot use rifle rounds. Crew members with shared weapon types are often the best source; hostile forces may carry weapons the character cannot benefit from.

*Consequence:* situational — taking from a fallen crew member is morally and tactically charged. Taking from hostile forces during active engagement is dangerous.

**Salvaged Components / Reload.** Manual reloading of spent casings or extraction of usable rounds from damaged ammunition. Restores Ammo one step up. Time: 5 to 15 minutes. Engineering skill check.

Most useful when other options are unavailable. The product is often less reliable — at the Warden's discretion, salvaged ammo may have a chance of jamming or misfire on first use.

*Consequence:* time-intensive. A character salvaging ammo cannot be doing much else. Reliability may be lower than fresh stock.

**Weapon Swap.** Abandon the empty weapon and pick up a different one. Not technically recovery — replacement. The new weapon comes with its own Ammo die at whatever level the previous user left it.

Common sources: fallen crew, hostile forces, weapon racks, security lockers.

*Consequence:* the character now carries a weapon they may not be trained on. Skill rolls at penalty if the character is not familiar with the weapon type.

## 14.5 Stims and Medical Supply Recovery

Medical supplies are usually scarce. Recovery is often slower and more involved than other resources.

**Med Bay Restock.** A station's primary medical facility. Restores Stims/Medkit to default. Time: 5 to 10 minutes — inventory, selection, packing.

Often Officer clearance. Locked during quarantine protocols or biohazard alerts. Med bays may be raided, contaminated, or occupied by a medical emergency that prevents access.

*Consequence:* the supplies the character takes are not available to the crew. A medic without supplies cannot treat injuries elsewhere on the station.

**First Aid Station.** Smaller medical caches distributed throughout the station — corridors, work areas, life pods. Restores Stims/Medkit one step up. Time: 1 to 2 minutes.

Generally Crew clearance. Designed for emergency self-treatment. Stocks may be depleted in active situations or by previous users.

*Consequence:* none beyond depletion.

**Looted Kit.** Take medical supplies from another character or fallen body. Restoration depends on what they carried — typically one step up. Time: 1 to 3 minutes.

Crew members and dedicated medical personnel carry better supplies than typical crew. Hostile forces rarely carry useful medical equipment.

*Consequence:* situational. The body of a fallen crew member offering medical supplies the character desperately needs is a quietly devastating scene.

**Crew Administered.** A medic comes to the character's location and treats them. Restores Stims/Medkit to default *and* provides direct medical care that the character cannot self-administer. Time: 10 to 30 minutes. Both the character and the medic are stationary.

Requires the medic to be able to reach the character — comms, clearance, working corridors. The medic is exposed during travel and during treatment.

*Consequence:* both parties exposed. Strong narrative moment. The medic's section is empty during their absence; if a medical emergency occurs there, no one is present to respond.

## 14.6 Rations Recovery

Rations are the slowest-changing resource. Recovery options are varied.

**Galley / Mess.** Standard food preparation area. Restores Rations to default. Time: 15 to 30 minutes — preparation, eating, hydration. The character is committed to a sit-down meal; this is a recovery scene, not a quick action.

Generally Crew clearance. May be unavailable during quarantine, atmospheric loss, or extended power loss (refrigeration matters).

*Consequence:* extended time investment. Often the only way to reset rations to default.

**Emergency Rations Cache.** Stored ration packs distributed throughout the station — life pods, emergency kits, specific stations. Restores Rations one step up. Time: 1 to 2 minutes.

Generally accessible, often unmarked or hidden. The character may need to know where caches are kept, or may discover them by chance.

*Consequence:* rations from caches are bland, often unpleasant. Crew Stress may not benefit as it would from a proper meal.

**Field Rations / Salvaged Supplies.** Ration packs found on bodies, in abandoned gear, or in supply containers in unexpected locations. Restoration: one step up, sometimes more if the find is significant. Time: 2 to 5 minutes.

May or may not be in good condition. Older salvage may be expired, contaminated, or simply unappetizing.

*Consequence:* situational. Long-spoiled rations may cause illness; the oracle decides on questionable cases.

**Synthesized From Station Materials.** Long-term recovery from raw materials — hydroponics, protein synthesizer, water reclamation. Restores Rations one step up. Time: significant — typically a full shift (8 hours) for the system to produce usable food.

Requires functional facility, power, and (often) Engineering skill check to operate or correct the system. Usually a station-wide effort, not solo.

*Consequence:* time. A character relying on synthesized food has hours of waiting between meals. Useful for extended siege scenarios where galleys are inaccessible.

## 14.7 Recovery Under Pressure

Recovery rarely happens under ideal conditions. The character needs supplies *because* something has gone wrong. Several common pressure scenarios:

**Active threat.** The character is being pursued, or a threat is in the vicinity. Recovery options requiring sustained stationary time (Recharge Station, Med Bay restock, Galley meal) are dangerous. Emergency packs and quick swaps are preferred.

**Lockdown.** Station-wide protocols may seal off recovery locations. The character cannot reach the med bay, the galley, or the recharge station. Field methods become primary.

**Compromise.** Recovery locations the character thought were available may be reported as unavailable by a compromised AI — or vice versa. Cross-checking with crew or direct observation is necessary.

**Multiple resources low.** A character with low Battery, O2, and Stims simultaneously must prioritize. The most critical resource for the next scene comes first; others must wait.

**Crew unavailable.** Crew Transfer methods require functional comms and willing crew. When the crew is silent, busy, or themselves compromised, these methods are off the table.

The character cannot expect to recover everything. They choose what to recover, what to ration, and what to do without.

## 14.8 Recovery And The Web

Recovery interacts with every system in this manual.

- **Sections** must have power for many recovery options to work.
- **Doors** gate access to recovery locations.
- **Clearance** restricts which recovery locations the character can use.
- **Comms** is required for crew-assisted recovery.
- **AI** may report recovery locations as unavailable, possibly accurately or possibly under compromise.
- **Hot-Wire** can sometimes substitute for missing access, but cannot replace consumed supplies.

A station whose systems are degrading is a station where recovery becomes harder over time. The first hour, the recharge station works fine. The third hour, that section is in Brownout and the recharge takes longer. The sixth hour, the section is Dead, and the recharge is impossible. The character must move to other recovery options or leave the station.

This is intentional. Survival is a sliding window. The systems that support life require continuous functioning of the systems that surround them. When those start to fail, the character's options narrow.

The Crew Manual is, ultimately, a map of how a station fails. Recovery is what the character does in the gaps.
